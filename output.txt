NOTE ON THE ORIGINAL CODE
  The original artifact used T = 1.7 (hardcoded). This made ALL metrics worse:
  ΔECE = +0.056, ΔNLL = +0.071, ΔBrier = +0.023. The reason: the model is
  overconfident (T* < 1 needed), and applying T = 1.7 > 1 pushes probabilities
  further toward 0.5 — the opposite of the required correction. Calibration
  applied in the wrong direction is worse than no calibration.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CROSS-SCENARIO OBSERVATIONS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. T* direction encodes regime. T*=0.193 (overconf), T*=4.391 (underconf),
   T*=0.588 (imbalance + overconf). T* alone tells you which regime without
   needing to inspect every bin first.

2. ECE and NLL diverge under underconfidence. ECE drops 94% while NLL drops
   only 16%. This divergence is a signal: NLL's residual reflects discriminative
   information that was never present in the logit signal. Calibration cannot
   create information that the model never encoded.

3. Class imbalance is the only scenario where T* fails structurally.
   ECE drops only 22%. Bins 0.5-1.0 are MORE overconfident after calibration.
   T* is globally optimal (minimises NLL) yet makes per-bin reliability worse
   in the high-confidence range. A global scalar cannot resolve a base-rate
   mismatch that operates differently across bins.

4. A hardcoded T=1.7 would worsen overconfidence by 5.6 ECE points. T* must
   be optimised, not assumed. The sign of the correction matters as much as
   the magnitude.
